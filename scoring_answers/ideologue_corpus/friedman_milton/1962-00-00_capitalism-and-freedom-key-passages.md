# Capitalism and Freedom (1962) — key verbatim passages

Compiled from the EconLib #EconlibReads discussion guide, which quotes the 40th Anniversary Edition with page citations.

## Chapter 1: The Relation Between Economic Freedom and Political Freedom

- "How can we benefit from the promise of government while avoiding the threat to freedom?" (p. 2)
- "a society which is socialist cannot be democratic, in the sense of guaranteeing individual freedom" (p. 8)
- The market is "a system of proportional representation" (p. 15)

## Chapter 2: The Role of Government in a Free Society

- "If the end does not justify the means, what does?" (p. 22)
- "…private monopolies may be the least of the evils" (p. 28)
- "…neighborhood effects may justify a city park; they do not justify a national park…" (p. 31)

## Chapter 3: The Control of Money

- "'Full employment' and 'economic growth' have in the past few decades become primary excuses for widening the extent of government intervention in economic affairs." (chapter opening)
- "The need for the dispersal of power raises an especially difficult problem in the field of money." (p. 38)
- The Great Depression "was produced by government mismanagement rather than by any inherent instability of the private economy." (p. 38)
- "…the System's failure was a failure of will, not of power." (p. 48)

## Chapter 4: International Financial and Trade Agreements

- "We preach publicly the virtues of free trade; yet we have been forced by the inexorable pressure of the balance of payments to move in the opposite direction and there is great danger that we shall move still farther." (p. 66)
- "Being in favor of floating exchange rates does not mean being in favor of unstable exchange rates." (p. 69)

## Chapter 6: The Role of Government in Education

- "Our present school system, far from equalizing opportunity, very likely does the opposite." (pp. 92-93)
- "The subsidization of institutions rather than of people has led to an indiscriminate subsidization of all activities appropriate for the state to subsidize." (p. 100)
- "If it is appropriate for the state to say that individuals may not discriminate in employment because of color or race or religion, then it is equally appropriate for the state, provided a majority can be found to vote that way, to say that individuals must discriminate in employment on the basis of color or race or religion." (p. 113)

## Chapter 7: Capitalism and Discrimination

- Of minority groups: "They have tended to attribute to capitalism the residual restrictions they experience rather than to recognize that the free market has been the major factor enabling these restrictions to be as small as they are." (chapter opening)

## Chapter 8: Monopoly and the Social Responsibility of Business and Labor

- "If businessmen do have a social responsibility other than making maximum profits for stockholders, how are they to know what it is?" (p. 133)

## Chapter 9: Occupational Licensure

- "…certification without licensure is a half-way house that maintains a good deal of protection against monopolization." (p. 149)

## Chapter 10: The Distribution of Income

- "What is the justification for state intervention to promote equality?" (p. 161)
- "Payment in accordance with product may be necessary to achieve true equality of treatment." (p. 162)

## Chapter 11: Social Welfare Measures

- On minimum wages: "This case is in one respect very much like public housing. In both, the people who are helped are visible…" (p. 181)

## Chapter 12: The Alleviation of Poverty

- "The liberal will therefore distinguish between equality of rights and equality of opportunity, on the one hand, and material equality or equality of outcome on the other." (p. 195)

## Conclusion

- "In the 1920's and 1930's, intellectuals in the United States were overwhelmingly persuaded that capitalism was a defective system inhibiting economic well-being and thereby freedom, and that the future lay in a greater measure of deliberate control by political authorities over economic affairs." (chapter opening)
- "state help kills self-help" (p. 201, quoting Dicey)
